Quick Decision Framework
🔴 Hemorrhagic Shock / Trauma
  • Control bleeding FIRST (damage control)
  • Permissive hypotension target MAP 50–65 until surgical control
  • Massive transfusion: pRBC : FFP : PLT = 1:1:1
  • Limit crystalloid — each additional liter increases mortality
🟠 Distributive Shock (Sepsis)
  • Crystalloid bolus 30 mL/kg in first 3 hours (SSC 2021)
  • Prefer balanced solutions (RL, PlasmaLyte) over NS
  • Reassess frequently — avoid fluid overload
  • Add vasopressors if no response after 1–2 L bolus
🟡 Hypovolemic (Non-hemorrhagic)
  • Isotonic crystalloid as initial resuscitation
  • Consider etiology: vomiting → NS; diarrhea → RL; burns → Parkland
  • Replace ongoing losses with matched fluid type
🟢 Maintenance / Non-urgent
  • D5 0.45% NaCl + KCl for standard adult maintenance
  • Rate: 25–30 mL/kg/day (adjust for insensible losses)
  • Avoid NS for maintenance — risk hyperchloremic acidosis
⚠️ Rule of Thumb: For every 1 L of crystalloid given, only ~250 mL remains intravascular after 30–60 min. Colloids or blood products are far more efficient for volume expansion.

HYPO

D5W — 5% Dextrose in Water

▶
Key Concept

D5W is metabolically equivalent to free water — once glucose is metabolized, only water remains. It distributes equally throughout all body water (ICF + ECF). Only ~8% stays intravascular.

Indications ✅
  • Hypernatremia correction (true free water deficit)
  • Drug dilution / vehicle (adenosine, amiodarone, nitroglycerin)
  • Hypoglycemia (combined with dextrose boost)
  • Maintenance when Na is elevated
Avoid ❌
  • Resuscitation — ineffective volume expander
  • TBI / stroke (worsens cerebral edema)
  • Diabetes (worsens hyperglycemia)
  • Hyponatremia
🚫 NEVER use D5W as a resuscitation fluid. Its intravascular expansion is negligible. In TBI, it redistributes water into brain tissue — can be fatal.

SYNTHETIC

HES — Hydroxyethyl Starch (Voluven, Tetraspan)

▶
🚫 CONTRAINDICATED in sepsis and critical illness. Multiple trials (CHEST, 6S) showed increased AKI and mortality. EMA restricted use; FDA requires black box warning.
Remaining / Limited Indications
  • Perioperative volume expansion in non-septic surgical patients (limited use)
  • Where rapid volume expansion is needed and other options unavailable
Contraindications ❌
  • Sepsis / septic shock (absolute contraindication)
  • Renal impairment / AKI
  • Coagulopathy / bleeding disorders
  • Intracranial / intraspinal surgery
  • Liver failure
HES impairs von Willebrand factor, prolongs aPTT, and deposits in renal tubular cells — causing osmotic nephropathy. Still available in Indonesia but use with extreme caution.
SYNTHETIC

Gelatin-based Colloids (Gelafundin, Haemaccel)

▶
Overview

Gelatin colloids are derived from bovine collagen. They have shorter duration than HES (~3–4 hours) and lower oncotic pressure. Less evidence of AKI than HES, but safety data are less robust than crystalloids.

PropertyValue
Oncotic pressure~20–26 mmHg
Duration3–4 hours
Coagulation effectMild dilutional
Anaphylaxis risk~0.35% (higher than albumin)
Indications
  • Short-term volume expansion in surgical / perioperative settings
  • Bridge to blood products in hemorrhage when blood not immediately available
⚠️ Haemaccel contains Ca²⁺ — do NOT co-infuse with blood products (risk of clotting in line). Gelafundin is Ca²⁺-free.
POLYSACCHARIDE

Dextran (Dextran 40 / Dextran 70)

▶

Polysaccharide colloids. Largely replaced by other agents. Dextran 40 used primarily as anti-thrombotic / rheological agent, not primarily for volume expansion.

Historical / Current Uses
  • Dextran 40: Peripheral vascular disease, microvascular surgery (anti-sludging)
  • Dextran 70: Volume expansion (largely replaced)
Cautions ❌
  • Anaphylaxis risk (pre-treat with Dextran 1 — "hapten inhibitor")
  • Interferes with cross-matching — draw blood BEFORE administration
  • Coagulopathy at high doses
  • Renal tubular obstruction with Dextran 40

PCC

Prothrombin Complex Concentrate (Octaplex, Beriplex)

▶

4-factor PCC contains Factors II, VII, IX, X and Proteins C & S. Preferred over FFP for urgent anticoagulation reversal — faster, lower volume, no thaw delay.

Indications ✅
  • Emergency warfarin reversal (INR >4 with or without bleeding)
  • Intracranial hemorrhage on anticoagulation
  • Life-threatening bleeding on Vitamin K antagonists
Warfarin reversal: INR 2–4: 25 IU/kg INR 4–6: 35 IU/kg INR >6: 50 IU/kg Max dose: 3000 IU (150 mL) Always co-administer Vitamin K 10 mg IV slow infusion
💡 PCC reverses warfarin in minutes vs hours for FFP. For life-threatening hemorrhage (ICH), PCC + Vitamin K is the standard of care.

SEPSIS

🦠 Septic Shock

▶
SSC 2021 Recommendations
  • Initial: 30 mL/kg IV crystalloid within 3 hours (strong recommendation)
  • Prefer balanced crystalloids (RL or PlasmaLyte) over NS
  • Reassess after each 500 mL bolus (dynamic fluid responsiveness testing)
  • Add norepinephrine if MAP <65 despite 1–2 L fluid
  • Albumin as adjunct if large volumes of crystalloid needed
Fluid Responsiveness Assessment
  • Passive Leg Raise (PLR): ↑CO >10% = fluid responsive (best dynamic test)
  • Pulse pressure variation (PPV): >13% = fluid responsive (mechanical ventilation only)
  • POCUS IVC: collapsibility index >50% suggests hypovolemia
  • Mini fluid challenge: 100–200 mL rapid bolus → measure response
⚠️ Fluid overload in sepsis = worse outcomes. SMART, BASICS trials favor balanced crystalloids. Do not reflexively give all 30 mL/kg if patient improves earlier — stop when no longer fluid responsive.

BURNS

🔥 Burns Resuscitation

▶
Parkland Formula
4 mL × weight (kg) × %TBSA burned = Total volume in 24h (RL) ½ given in first 8h (from time of burn, not arrival) ½ given over next 16h Modified Brooke: 2 mL × kg × %TBSA (RL) [less fluid-aggressive] Children: Galveston formula (5000 mL/m² burned + 2000 mL/m² BSA)
Monitoring Targets
  • Urine output 0.5–1 mL/kg/h in adults (gold standard)
  • MAP >65 mmHg
  • Adjust rate hourly based on UO — do NOT give extra boluses
⚠️ Parkland often leads to "fluid creep" (overshoot). Consider albumin after 8–12h (0.5 mL/kg/h of 5% albumin) to reduce total crystalloid load in large burns (>40% TBSA).
TBI

🧠 Traumatic Brain Injury

▶
Fluid Principles in TBI
  • Maintain euvolemia — avoid hypovolemia AND fluid overload
  • Use isotonic fluids ONLY — NS 0.9% preferred
  • AVOID hypotonic fluids (D5W, 0.45% NaCl) — worsen cerebral edema
  • AVOID albumin (SAFE trial — increased mortality in TBI)
  • Target MAP ≥80 mmHg (CPP ≥60 mmHg) — use vasopressors if needed
  • For elevated ICP: Mannitol 0.25–1 g/kg IV OR 3% NaCl 2–3 mL/kg

LIVER

🫁 Liver Failure / SBP / Cirrhosis

▶
Fluid Principles
  • Avoid NS — sodium retention worsens ascites
  • Albumin is the colloid of choice in cirrhosis
  • Restrict sodium in IVF (if Na restriction needed)
  • Avoid fluid overload — risk of renal failure and dilutional hyponatremia
Specific Protocols
SBP: Albumin 1.5 g/kg Day 1 + 1 g/kg Day 3 (reduces HRS from 30% to 10%) LVP (>5L drained): Albumin 6–8 g per liter removed (use 20%) (prevents post-paracentesis circulatory dysfunction) HRS Type 1: Terlipressin 1–2 mg IV q6h + Albumin 1 g/kg/day (target: reversal of HRS or bridge to transplant)

CARDIAC

❤️ Heart Failure / Cardiogenic Shock

▶
🚫 Fluid boluses are generally CONTRAINDICATED in decompensated heart failure. Volume loading worsens pulmonary edema and is rarely beneficial.
Principles
  • Use diuretics (furosemide) — not fluid boluses — for decompensated HF
  • Cardiogenic shock: optimize preload (may be euvolemic or hypervolemic)
  • Small fluid challenge (250 mL) ONLY if clinically hypovolemic
  • Use vasopressors/inotropes, not fluid, for cardiogenic shock
  • POCUS-guided: assess LV function, IVC, B-lines (pulmonary edema) before any fluid
Drug Restriction for Fluid
  • Drug diluent: minimize volume — use concentrated preparations
  • IV antibiotics: consider smaller volumes if volume-restricted
  • Maintenance: restrict to 1–1.5 L/day if fluid overloaded
RENAL

🫘 Acute Kidney Injury / CKD

▶
Fluid Selection Principles
  • Prefer balanced crystalloids (RL, PlasmaLyte) — NS increases AKI risk
  • Avoid HES — causes osmotic nephropathy
  • Monitor K⁺ — avoid K⁺-containing fluids in hyperkalemia
  • Restrict fluid in oliguria/anuric AKI — avoid overload
  • Use small boluses (250 mL) and reassess — dynamic fluid tolerance
⚠️ KDIGO: In AKI prevention, isotonic crystalloid (not NS) is preferred. Avoid nephrotoxic iodinated contrast + NS in at-risk patients. Pre-hydration with NS or NaHCO₃ reduces contrast-induced nephropathy.
Contrast Nephropathy Prevention
High-risk patients (CKD, DM, CHF): NS 0.9%: 1 mL/kg/h × 12h before + 12h after contrast OR NaHCO₃ 1.26%: 3 mL/kg/h × 1h before + 1 mL/kg/h × 6h after
OB

🤰 Obstetric Emergencies

▶
Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH)
Crystalloid: 1–2 L RL rapid bolus Blood transfusion: pRBC if EBL >1000 mL or Hgb <8 g/dL MTP 1:1:1 if massive PPH TXA: 1 g IV within 3h of delivery (WOMAN trial — reduces death from bleeding) Fibrinogen: target >2 g/L (higher than non-obstetric)
Pre-eclampsia / Eclampsia
  • Restrict IV fluid — risk of pulmonary edema (leaky capillaries)
  • Maintenance: 80–100 mL/h maximum
  • MgSO₄ seizure prophylaxis: 4–6 g IV load + 1–2 g/h infusion
  • If pulmonary edema develops: furosemide, not more fluid
⚠️ Pre-eclampsia: Do NOT give aggressive fluid resuscitation — these patients are already edematous. Max fluid rate ~80 mL/h. Antihypertensives + MgSO₄ are the primary therapy.
ELDERLY

👴 Elderly / Frail Patients

▶
  • Reduced tolerance for fluid overload — smaller boluses (250 mL) and frequent reassessment
  • Higher risk of delirium with both under-hydration and over-hydration
  • Avoid aggressive sodium correction (higher risk ODS)
  • Lower transfusion threshold may be appropriate: consider Hgb <8 (cardiac comorbidities)
  • Use POCUS to guide fluid decisions when uncertain
💡 Ovarian Hyperstimulation Syndrome (OHSS) is an iatrogenic complication of ovarian stimulation (ART/IVF). It involves massive third-spacing of fluid driven by VEGF-mediated capillary leak — similar to sepsis physiology but in a different context. Fluid management is counterintuitive and nuanced.

FLUID Rx

💧 Fluid Management in OHSS

▶
First-Line: Albumin (Key Agent)

Albumin is the preferred fluid in OHSS — it expands intravascular volume while minimizing third-space expansion. High oncotic pressure reduces capillary leak-driven fluid shift.

Albumin 25% (Salt-poor): 50–100 g IV (200–400 mL) over 4h → Repeat every 4–12h based on UO, Hct, and symptoms Albumin 5%: 200–300 mL IV if 25% unavailable Target: Hct <45%, UO >30 mL/h, resolution of oliguria
Crystalloid Use
  • Use isotonic balanced crystalloid (RL preferred) for maintenance — NOT NS (avoid Na load worsening hyponatremia)
  • Limit to 1–1.5 L/day if ascites is severe — crystalloid leaks into third space rapidly
  • Small boluses (250 mL) for symptomatic hypotension only — reassess after each
Colloids
  • HES: avoid — risk of renal impairment and coagulopathy
  • Gelatin (Gelafundin): acceptable alternative if albumin unavailable — 500 mL IV
  • Dextran 70: used historically but now mostly replaced by albumin
What to AVOID
  • Large volume crystalloid — worsens ascites and hyponatremia
  • Diuretics (furosemide) — CONTRAINDICATED unless pulmonary edema — will worsen hemoconcentration and thrombosis risk
  • Paracentesis to treat oliguria — does NOT reliably improve UO; only indicated for respiratory compromise or intractable pain
⚠️ Cabergoline 0.5 mg/day PO × 8 days (started at hCG trigger) reduces early OHSS by downregulating VEGFR-2. Not a fluid treatment, but important to know in the management context.

ICU

🏥 Critical OHSS — ICU Management

▶
  • Paracentesis: indicated for respiratory compromise (large ascites/pleural effusion), severe pain, or refractory oliguria — ultrasound-guided preferred
  • Pleural tap: only if respiratory failure despite paracentesis
  • Dialysis / CRRT: for refractory AKI with fluid overload
  • ARDS management: lung-protective ventilation if mechanical ventilation required
  • Dopamine (low dose): historical use for renal perfusion — not currently recommended as standard
Paracentesis in OHSS: Drain ascites under US guidance No albumin replacement needed per liter (unlike cirrhosis) — albumin is being given for volume expansion, not replacement Re-accumulation expected — repeat as needed for symptoms
💡 OHSS is self-limiting if not pregnant — resolves in 10–14 days. If pregnancy occurs (especially multifetal), it may be prolonged and more severe due to sustained hCG stimulation.
🚨 Anaphylaxis is a medical emergency. First-line treatment is EPINEPHRINE — not antihistamines, not steroids, not fluids. IV fluid is a critical adjunct for hypotension but must not delay epinephrine.
FIRST LINE

💉 Immediate Treatment — Epinephrine First

▶
EPINEPHRINE (ADRENALINE) — Give FIRST, always ────────────────────────────────────────────── IM (preferred route): 0.3–0.5 mg (0.3–0.5 mL of 1:1000) → Anterolateral thigh (vastus lateralis) → May repeat every 5–15 min PRN → Children: 0.01 mg/kg IM (max 0.5 mg) IV (anaphylactic shock / cardiac arrest): → 0.1 mg (1 mL of 1:10,000) slow IV push → OR infusion: 0.1–1 mcg/kg/min titrate to response → Prepare: 1 mg epi in 100 mL NS = 10 mcg/mL Nebulized (stridor only, not systemic): 5 mg (5 mL of 1:1000) via nebulizer
FLUIDS

💧 Fluid Resuscitation in Anaphylaxis

▶
Why Fluids Are Needed

Anaphylaxis causes massive vasodilation + capillary leak — leading to distributive shock. Up to 35% of intravascular volume can shift to tissues within minutes. IV fluid corrects relative hypovolemia while epinephrine acts on vasculature.

Fluid Protocol
ADULTS: 0.9% NS or RL: 1–2 L IV rapid bolus (as fast as possible) Repeat 500 mL boluses every 5–10 min, reassess after each Total: up to 4–6 L may be needed in refractory shock CHILDREN: NS or RL: 20 mL/kg IV bolus over 5–10 min Repeat up to 3× (60 mL/kg total) if still shocked

REFRACTORY

🔄 Refractory Anaphylaxis / Biphasic Reaction

▶
Refractory Anaphylaxis (No Response to 2–3 Epi Doses)
IV Epinephrine infusion: 0.1–1 mcg/kg/min (titrate to MAP ≥65) Add Norepinephrine: 0.1–0.5 mcg/kg/min if hypotension persists Consider Vasopressin: 0.03 units/min Glucagon (if β-blocker): 1–5 mg IV then 5–15 mcg/min infusion Methylene Blue: 1.5–2 mg/kg IV over 20 min (last resort, vasoplegia)
Biphasic Anaphylaxis
  • Recurrence of symptoms 4–12 hours after initial resolution (up to 72h reported)
  • Incidence: ~5–20% of anaphylaxis cases
  • Observe all severe anaphylaxis patients for minimum 6–8 hours in ED
  • If severe index reaction or persistent symptoms: admit for 24h observation
  • Discharge with Epipen prescription and anaphylaxis action plan
Positioning Matters
  • Hypotension: supine with legs raised (like PLR)
  • Respiratory distress: sit upright or semi-recumbent
  • Pregnant patient: left lateral decubitus + leg raise
  • Cardiac arrest: CPR — epinephrine still first-line

DANGER

⚠️ Signs of Fluid Overload

▶
Clinical Features
  • New or worsening peripheral edema
  • Pulmonary edema — dyspnea, SpO₂ drop, crackles, B-lines on POCUS
  • Rising CVP, distended neck veins
  • Abdominal compartment syndrome — rising bladder pressure, abdominal distension
  • Worsening oxygenation requiring increased FiO₂
Fluid Overload Management
  • Stop further fluid administration
  • Furosemide 40–80 mg IV (if euvolemic or hypervolemic)
  • Target negative fluid balance — "de-resuscitation"
  • CRRT in oliguric AKI with fluid overload
  • Albumin 20% + furosemide ("salt-poor albumin strategy") in hypoalbuminaemic patients
⚠️ Positive cumulative fluid balance is independently associated with worse outcomes in ICU patients (sepsis, ARDS). Aim for zero to negative balance by Day 3 if hemodynamically stable.
